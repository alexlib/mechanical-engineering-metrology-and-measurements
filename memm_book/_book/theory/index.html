2.1 Introduction

2.1.1 Results in the class: rod’s outer diameter is larger than the holder’s inner diameter

It’s quite common in practice to measure the outer diameter (OD) of a rod and the inner diameter (ID) of a holder and get numbers where the OD appears larger than the ID—even when the rod clearly fits! This seems paradoxical, but can be fully explained by understanding all sources of measurement uncertainty in caliper use. Let’s break down:

2.1.2 1. All Contributors to Uncertainty (from Caliper Case Studies and Engineers’ Insight)

Here’s a thorough uncertainty budget for both OD and ID measurements:

Source Typical Size Description
Resolution 0.02 mm–0.05 mm Smallest readable increment, half used for standard uncertainty1[^6]
Calibration Error 0.01 mm–0.03 mm Systematic offset if the caliper is not perfectly zeroed or calibrated2
Parallax/Reading 0.01 mm–0.02 mm Eye misalignment or misreading the scale
Jaw Alignment/Play 0.02 mm–0.05 mm Slight movement/bending when jaws close on an object3[^6]
Force of Measurement 0.01 mm–0.03 mm Squeezing the instrument too hard/not enough (outer or inner jaws)
Workpiece Imperfection 0.01 mm–0.05 mm Out-of-roundness, burrs, surface roughness, dirt
Operator Variation 0.03 mm–0.06 mm Different persons, repeated measures, even mood/technique differences[^6]
Temperature Effect 0.01 mm–0.03 mm Both object and caliper expand/contract, especially if not at 20°C4
Measurement Method 0.02 mm–0.05 mm “Pinching” for ID/OD can give different distortion (caliper flexibility)
Jaw Thickness Effect up to 0.10 mm Especially for small holes: inner jaws may “bridge” and undersize the ID[^6]

Key Insight:

  • OD (rod): Caliper bends less and fits snugly on a smooth, solid surface. Usually systematic error sources are a bit smaller.
  • ID (holder): Caliper inner jaws may bridge across slight imperfections or wear, are influenced by jaw thickness, and are less able to “reach” the true diameter (so will often underestimate the ID).[^6]

2.1.3 2. Total Combined Uncertainty (as Practiced)

For typical mechanical work, with a good-quality caliper and skilled operator:

  • Standard uncertainty for OD: ~0.05 mm
  • Standard uncertainty for ID: ~0.06 mm
  • Expanded uncertainty (95%, k=2):
    • OD: ±0.10 mm
    • ID: ±0.12 mm[^6]

This means actual size could be ahead or behind measured by up to these amounts! If the reported OD is, say, 20.05 mm and the ID is 20.00 mm, the uncertainties overlap: the rod may well fit (and the values are not truly contradictory).

2.1.4 3. Resolution vs. Real-World Fit

The caliper’s resolution is not the only thing that matters. All biases and uncertainty sources must be considered, and for tight fits, surface roughness, burrs, and measurement force can easily tilt results back and forth.

2.1.6 5. How to Explain Your Result

  • Why OD > ID in numbers? Because systematic under-measure of ID (jaw bridging) + random error + uncertainty all play a role. Within stated uncertainties, the two measured values are likely still compatible, and the physical fit confirms it. This is a classic, well-understood metrology challenge!
  • What to tell others: Report both the measured values and their uncertainties. Note that for tight fits, direct fit (assembly test) gives ultimate truth, not just the numbers.

2.1.6.1 Summary:

When you combine all sources of measurement error for caliper work, it’s normal for OD and ID results to numerically overlap within uncertainty, even if the average seems paradoxical. The best practice is always to report uncertainties alongside measurements and recognize the instrument’s physical limits.
